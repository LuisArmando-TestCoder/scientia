## emerging_theory_or_intuition

The 'Personhood' axiom is a semantic trap; the real conflict is the 'Compulsory Use of Body' vs. 'Right to Exist'. Even between two equal adults, one cannot be forced to donate blood or organs; therefore, personhood of the fetus does not automatically negate the justifiability of abortion.

## boolean_formula_to_natural_language

If a fetus is a person (A) and has adult rights (B), then abortion is justifiable (C) only if adult rights include the right to refuse the use of one's body for another's survival (D).

## conclusion

If personhood is granted at conception, a fetus gains the 'right to life' but not the 'right to the body of another,' meaning that because no person can be morally compelled to sacrifice their bodily integrity for another, abortion remains justifiable as the withdrawal of life-sustaining support. And for all this, abortion is morally justifiable as a matter of negative right (the right not to be used) rather than a positive right to kill.

## logical_tension

- **paradox**: The Right to Life Paradox: If a fetus has the rights of an adult, it cannot be killed, yet if the mother has the rights of an adult, she cannot be forced to serve as a biological host.
- **ambiguity**: Does 'right to life' imply a right to be born, or simply a right not to be 'murdered'?
- **useful_contradiction**: The fetus is a guest that is also an intruder; it is both 'self' and 'other'.

## reconclusion

Accepting the premise that a fetus is a person does not logically lead to the conclusion that abortion is unjustifiable, because the right to life does not include the right to use another's body without consent. Therefore, within a system of equal rights, abortion remains a justifiable exercise of bodily sovereignty. 

## preconceptual_reconclusion
