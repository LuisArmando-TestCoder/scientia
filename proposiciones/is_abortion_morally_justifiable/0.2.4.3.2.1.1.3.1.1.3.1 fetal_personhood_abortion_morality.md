## argument_reasons

- Identity preservation (the zygote is the same biological individual as the adult person), justifying the claim of inherent personhood.
- Potentiality argument (the capacity to become a person implies being a person), though it often lacks a clear distinction between 'potential being' and 'being with potential'.
- The absence of a non-arbitrary biological 'cutoff' point between conception and birth, leading to the use of conception as the default moral boundary.

- **nature**: Axiomatic identity claim

- **tension**: Conflict between biological continuity (being the same organism) and psychological discontinuity (the absence of mind/consciousness).
- **limit**: Dependent on the definition of 'person', which is a socially/philosophically constructed category, not a purely empirical one.

## conclusion

If 'personhood' is axiomatically derived from genetic uniqueness, then the fetus is a person; however, if it is derived from sentience or functional autonomy, it is not; and for all this, the morality of abortion cannot be solved by the biological fact of conception alone, as personhood is a philosophical overlay onto biological data. and for all this the statement is an uncollapsible metaphysical axiom.
