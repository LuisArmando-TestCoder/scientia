## input_semantic_node

Fetal personhood from conception in the context of abortion

## central_semantic_node

The Moral and Ontological Status of the Zygote/Fetus

## argument_reasons

- Identity Continuity: The argument that a single biological life begins at fertilization and continues until death.
- Potentiality: The argument that because a fetus can become an adult, it deserves the rights of an adult.
- Speciesism/Biological Membership: The claim that being genetically human is a sufficient condition for personhood.
- Absence of a non-arbitrary 'cutoff' point: The difficulty in defining a specific moment other than conception (e.g., birth, viability, heartbeat) that isn't philosophically contested.

## ontological_signature

- **nature**: Axiomatic moral proposition
- **function**: To establish a legal and ethical barrier against the termination of pregnancy.
- **domain**: Bioethics, Metaphysical Ontogeny, Jurisprudence.
- **form**: Linear-essentialist (A leads to B).
- **tension**: The conflict between biological 'human life' and the cognitive/relational definition of 'personhood'.
- **limit**: Depends on the prior acceptance of 'personhood' as an inherent rather than an earned or emergent property.

## preconceptual_conclusion

We can't agree if the tiny seed is a person yet because we can't agree on what makes someone a person: is it the instructions they carry (DNA) or is it what they can do (think/feel)?

## conclusion

If biological life is synonymous with moral personhood, then a fetus is a person, but since personhood involves social and cognitive dimensions not present at conception, as evidenced by the twinning paradox and lack of sentience, and for all this, the statement remains an uncollapsable axiomatic preference rather than an empirical fact.

## reconclusion

The claim that a fetus is a person from conception is a metaphysical anchor used to bypass the complexity of shifting biological stages; while biologically 'human', the leap to 'person' requires a value judgment that cannot be solved by biology alone, leading to an irresolvable conflict between autonomy and potentiality.

## preconceptual_reconclusion

The story is about when we decide to call someone 'one of us'. Because we don't agree on the rules for that name, the answer stays hidden in the middle.
