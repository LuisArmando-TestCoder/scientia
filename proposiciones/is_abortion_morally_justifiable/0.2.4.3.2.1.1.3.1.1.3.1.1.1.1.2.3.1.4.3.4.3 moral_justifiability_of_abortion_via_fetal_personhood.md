## conclusion

If we define a fetus as a person with full rights from the moment of conception, then the moral justifiability of abortion depends not on the fetus's lack of humanity but on whether one person is ever obligated to provide their body as life-support for another, and since no other legal or moral precedent requires an adult to donate organs or blood to another person against their will, the claim that fetal personhood automatically makes abortion unjustifiable is logically inconsistent with broader principles of bodily autonomy, and for all this the justifiability of abortion remains an unresolved conflict between two absolute rights-claims that cannot be collapsed without an axiomatic preference.

## reconclusion

The moral justifiability of abortion is not a singular truth but a relational equation; if personhood is granted to the fetus, the equation becomes a conflict of standing between two rights-bearing individuals where the pregnant individual's bodily sovereignty remains the primary site of agency. Therefore, personhood does not settle the debate; it merely upgrades its status to a high-level logical stalemate.
