## argument_reasons

- The logical structure assumes that if A (conception) equals B (personhood), then B (personhood) necessitates C (full moral rights).
- It presents a nested dependency where the moral justifiability of abortion depends entirely on the rejection of this equivalence.

## conclusion

If personhood is defined as an inherent quality starting at conception (A), then moral rights must logically extend to the fetus (B), and if B exists, it creates an irreconcilable conflict with the justifiability of ending its development (C), however, since A is an unproven metaphysical axiom, the conclusion remains tethered to belief systems rather than empirical proof, and for all this the statement cannot be irrefutably affirmed in a secular logical framework.

## logical_tension

- **paradox**: The Potentiality Paradox: A fetus is not yet what it is claimed to be (an adult), but it is treated as one based on what it will become.
- **ambiguity**: The term 'person' is used both biologically and legally/philosophically without clear demarcation.
- **useful_contradiction**: The coexistence of the fetus as both a 'part of the mother's body' and a 'distinct biological individual' generates the friction that fuels the entire debate.

## reconclusion

The claim that a fetus possesses the same moral rights as an adult is a deductive conclusion derived from the axiom of conception-based personhood; while logically valid within its own internal definitions, it remains epistemologically incomplete because the starting axiom (conception = personhood) is a matter of ontological preference rather than verifiable fact, leading to an irresolvable conflict with the axiom of bodily autonomy.
