## conclusion

If bodily autonomy is a fundamental right to self-governance, and if no individual has the right to use the body of another for survival without consent—as evidenced by the lack of forced organ donation laws—then the right to life does not inherently include the right to biological support from another, and for all this, the absolute claim of supersession is logically invalid within a framework of individual self-ownership. ## global_reevaluation

- **state**: false

## reconclusion

The claim that life always overrides autonomy fails because the right to life is fundamentally a right against interference, not a right to the biological resources of another. Therefore, abortion is morally justifiable when framed as the withdrawal of bodily support rather than the negation of a right to exist.

## preconceptual_reconclusion

Nobody has to lend their body to anyone else, even if that person really needs it to live.

## causal_chain

Individual personhood implies self-ownership as an axiomatic starting point for all rights. Self-ownership requires the power to exclude others from one's physical body. If the right to life is interpreted as a right to use another's body, self-ownership is destroyed. Because the destruction of self-ownership removes the basis for all rights, the right to life cannot be interpreted as a right to another's body. Consequently, removing a dependent entity from one's body is an exercise of self-ownership, not a violation of a foundational right to life, rendering the original premise of absolute supersession false.
