## conclusion

If biological life is distinct from personhood, and personhood is the source of rights, then the fetus's status is contingent on a subjective definition; however, even if the fetus is a person, the lack of a universal right to use another's body for survival (C) implies that abortion remains justifiable under the principle of bodily autonomy, and for all this, abortion is morally justifiable in contexts where the host's autonomy is prioritized over the guest's potential for life.

## reconclusion

The proposition 'a fetus has a right to life' is biologically true but meta-ethically insufficient to render abortion unjustifiable, as rights are not exercised in a vacuum but within a conflict of autonomous domains where the actualized rights of a conscious agent (the mother) take precedence over the potential rights of a developing entity (the fetus).

## preconceptual_reconclusion

Just because a tiny life is growing doesn't mean it can take over the big life that is carrying it, because being alive is not the same as having the right to stay in someone else's space without their permission.

## causal_chain

Biological inception creates a genetically unique human entity (A) -> This entity lacks independent metabolic agency (B) -> Moral rights are traditionally granted to independent agents (C) -> The fetus is nested within the mother's biological domain (D) -> A right to life is a negative right against interference, not a positive right to biological sustenance from another (E) -> Therefore, the mother's right to exclude entries from her bodily domain remains intact (F) -> For the fetus to have an absolute right to life, it would require a subversion of the mother's right to self-ownership (G) -> Since self-ownership is the foundation of all human rights, subverting it for the fetus creates a logical contradiction (H) -> Thus, the right to life of the fetus cannot supersede the autonomy of the mother in a consistent rights-based framework.
