## reconclusion

The identification of personhood with biological distinctness is an axiomatic choice designed to create a clear moral boundary; however, because personhood functions as a socio-legal status awarded based on varied criteria (sentience, viability, birth), the synonymy is a prescriptive claim rather than an ontological truth. Consequently, the moral justifiability of abortion remains an interference of competing rights (autonomy vs. life) that personhood alone cannot resolve.

## causal_chain

A distinct biological organism is defined by unique DNA. DNA determines the development of a human being. The argument assumes that being a human being at any stage is the same as being a person. Persons have a right to life. If a fetus is a person, it has a right to life. Abortion ends that life. Therefore, if the definition holds, abortion violates a person's rights. However, personhood often requires consciousness. A fetus does not have consciousness in early stages. This breaks the synonymy between 'organism' and 'person'. If they are not the same, the moral status of abortion depends on when the transition occurs. Since the transition point is not biological but philosophical, the synonymy is an unproven premise. Without an agreed premise, the conclusion of moral unjustifiability is logically valid within the closed system but not empirically settled in the global system.
