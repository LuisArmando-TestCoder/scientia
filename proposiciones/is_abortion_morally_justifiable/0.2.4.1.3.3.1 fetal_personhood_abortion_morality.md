## argument_reasons

- Biological Continuity: The argument that life begins at conception based on genetic uniqueness.
- Potentiality: The argument that a fetus is a 'potential' person and thus has rights.
- Sentience: The argument that personhood requires a level of consciousness or the ability to feel pain.
- Bodily Autonomy: The counter-argument that even if a fetus is a person, the mother's right to her body is primary.

If personhood is defined by cognitive function then the fetus is not a person, yet if it is defined by genetic potential it is, however since no universal consensus exists on this definition, the moral justifiability of abortion shifts to the only verifiable agent involved which is the mother, and for all this, fetal personhood remains a subjective axiom rather than a binary fact.

## causal_chain

Biological life begins at conception as a unique genetic entity -> Unique DNA is a necessary but not sufficient condition for moral personhood -> Personhood typically requires functional attributes like sentience or self-awareness -> A fetus develops these attributes on a gradient scale rather than a binary switch -> Because the 'switch' is arbitrary, different systems choose different points to start 'Rights' -> If the fetus is a person, abortion is a conflict of two persons' rights -> If the fetus is not a person, abortion is an exercise of bodily autonomy -> Since personhood cannot be empirically proven, the decision-making agency defaults to the individual whose biological systems are sustaining the entity, resulting in a pragmatic justification for autonomy despite ontological uncertainty.
