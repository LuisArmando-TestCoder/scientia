## boolean_formula_to_natural_language

## conclusion

If we define personhood strictly by genetic individuality, then rights are conferred at conception; however, since rights are socially negotiated based on sentience and agency, a biological-only definition creates a category error, and for all this the statement remains a contested axiom rather than a logical necessity.

## global_reevaluation

- **state**: self-referential
- **criteria**: The argument 'A fetus has rights because it is a person' and 'It is a person because it has rights' creates a circular epistemic loop that never leaves the initial subjective bias.

## reconclusion

The assertion of equal moral rights for a fetus is a top-down ontological imposition that ignores the bottom-up emergence of consciousness and social agency, leading to an irreconcilable conflict between biological existence and ethical subjectivity.

## preconceptual_reconclusion

Looking at the seed and the tree together, we see they are part of the same story, but because one is a promise and the other is a reality, we cannot say they are exactly the same thing right now.

## causal_chain

Biological life begins at fertilization creating a unique DNA sequence which defines a human being in a biological sense but moral rights are a social construct built upon the capacities of sentience and agency which a fetus does not yet possess until later stages of development thus creating a gap between being human and being a person as defined by social contracts which implies that assigning full adult rights to a fetus requires an axiomatic leap of faith that overrides the empirical difference in functional capability and social interaction therefore the initial claim is only true if one accepts a non-empirical definition of personhood as being synonymous with genetic existence.
