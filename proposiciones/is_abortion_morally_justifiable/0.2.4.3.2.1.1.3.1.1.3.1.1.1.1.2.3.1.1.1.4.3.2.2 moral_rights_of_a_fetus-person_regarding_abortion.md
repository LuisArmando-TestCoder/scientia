## conclusion

The syllogism collapses into a logical loop because the premise 'Person = Same Rights' is used to define 'Fetus', ignoring that even adult rights do not grant the privilege of biological squatting, and for all this, fetal personhood is an insufficient metric for determining the moral justifiability of abortion.

## logical_tension

- **paradox**: The 'Right to Life' paradox: No person has the right to use another's organs without consent, even if they need them to live.
- **ambiguity**: Does 'Adult Rights' include the duty to be a life-support system?
- **useful_contradiction**: A fetus is simultaneously a distinct life and a part of the mother's biological process.

## analogous_reorganization

- Organ donation: You cannot force an adult to give blood to another adult, even if the second adult will die without it.
- Property law: The rights of a guest (fetus) vs. the rights of the homeowner (mother).

## implications

- Moral justifiability must shift from 'Status of Fetus' to 'Consent of Carrier'.
- Legal systems must reconcile individual autonomy with the sanctity of life as separate, often conflicting, vectors.

The assessment that fetal personhood determines the morality of abortion is logically incomplete; a rigorous analysis requires the inclusion of the maternal right to bodily self-determination as a primary axiom, which often supersedes the negative right (right not to be killed) with a positive right (right to exclude others from one's body).
