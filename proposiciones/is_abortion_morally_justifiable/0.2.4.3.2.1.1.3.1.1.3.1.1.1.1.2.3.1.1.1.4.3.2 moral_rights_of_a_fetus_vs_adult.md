## reconclusion

The claim of moral equivalence between a fetus and an adult rests on an ontological essentialism that collapses when subjected to functional analysis or the priority of established bodily autonomy, rendering the proposition a subjective belief-state rather than a universal logical truth.

## preconceptual_reconclusion

## causal_chain

Starting from the biological event of conception where a unique DNA sequence is formed, an observer assigns the label of 'person' to this organism based on the belief in essential potentiality; this label functions as a bridge to import adult deontology into the womb; if this bridge holds, then any act of termination is framed as a violation of an equivalent set of rights; however, if rights are contingent upon social birth or consciousness, the bridge collapses; legal history and the biological reality of bodily gestation introduce a mechanical conflict between the containment vessel (the mother) and the entity contained (the fetus), which means that even if the fetus is a person, its right to life does not legally or logically compel the mother to provide life support against her will, as no adult is forced to donate organs or blood to another adult even to save their life, meaning the parity of rights actually leads back to the permissibility of disconnection. For all this, the equivalence of moral rights is a semantic assignment that fails to account for the physical reality of biological dependency.
