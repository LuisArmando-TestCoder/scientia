## input_semantic_node

Fetal Personhood & Abortion

## central_semantic_node

Ontological and Moral Status of the Fetus as a Person

## argument_reasons

- Biological Continuity: The claim that a unique genetic identity at conception establishes personhood.
- Moral Equivalence: The deduction that if a fetus is a person, it possesses an absolute right to life equal to an adult.
- Justifiability Context: The premise is used to restrict the moral permissibility of abortion by framing it as the termination of a person.

## ontological_signature

- **nature**: Axiomatic moral claim
- **function**: Establishing a legal and ethical boundary for life and rights.
- **domain**: Bioethics, Ontology, Jurisprudence
- **form**: Hierarchical normative dependency
- **tension**: Potentiality vs. Actuality; The rights of the fetus vs. the bodily autonomy of the pregnant person.
- **limit**: Depends on the definition of 'person', which is not a biological fact but a social/metaphysical construct.

## formula_dictionary

## boolean_formula_of_the_argument

(A && B) === C && (C -> D)

## why_the_formula_structure_is_incorrect

The formula assumes that moral justifiability (D) depends solely on the status of the fetus, ignoring the competing rights of the pregnant person (Self-Defense/Bodily Autonomy).

## boolean_argument_reformulation

((A && B) && !SelfDefense) -> D

## boolean_formula_to_natural_language

If a fetus is a person from conception and possesses adult moral rights, and there are no overriding rights like bodily integrity, then abortion is morally unjustifiable.

## conclusion

If personhood is defined by genetic uniqueness, then a fetus is a person, which implies abortion is the termination of a person; however, if personhood requires consciousness or social interaction, the fetus is not yet a person, and for all this, the moral status of abortion is contingent on the arbitrary selection of an ontological starting point that cannot be derived solely from biological observations.

## logical_tension

- **paradox**: A fetus is biologically a human life but legally and socially often a 'potential' person, existing in a state of 'becoming'.
- **ambiguity**: The exact moment when a 'cluster of cells' becomes a 'being with interests'.
- **useful_contradiction**: A fetus is both 'part' of the mother's body and a 'distinct' genetic entity.

## implications

- Resting the argument on 'conception' necessitates a total ban on many forms of contraception, creating a causal chain that limits reproductive freedom.
- Granting 'personhood' to a fetus creates a zero-sum conflict between two 'persons' sharing one body, which has no parallel in established law.

## reconclusion

The statement 'a fetus is a person from conception' is a foundational axiom of a specific moral system, not a universal truth. Logic dictates that if this axiom is accepted, the rights of the fetus are maximized; however, the axiom itself is subject to 'computational irreducibility' as it pertains to the subjective valuation of life.
