## central_semantic_node

Ontological and Moral Equivalence of Fetuses and Adults

## argument_reasons

- Biological Continuity: The argument assumes that because a fetus is biologically human and carries a unique genetic code from conception, it possesses the essential properties of a person.
- Potentiality Principle: The justification that a fetus will become an adult implies it already possesses the rights associated with that future state.
- Moral Absolutism: The claim relies on the premise that moral rights are inherent to biological life rather than dependent on cognitive capacities or social interactions.

## ontological_signature

- **nature**: Axiomatic moral claim
- **function**: To establish a legal and ethical prohibition of abortion by equating fetal life with adult life.
- **domain**: Bioethics, Meta-ethics, Legal Philosophy
- **form**: Recursive syllogism
- **tension**: Conflicts with the bodily autonomy of the pregnant individual and the gradualist view of neurological development.
- **limit**: Depends entirely on the definition of 'personhood', which is not empirically verifiable but philosophically assigned.

## conclusion

If personhood is defined by biological human identity then a fetus is a person, if a person has an absolute right to life then the fetus has an absolute right to life, if this right is equal to an adult's then abortion is prohibited, yet since bodily autonomy is also defined as an absolute right for adults a direct contradiction occurs where one person's right to life requires the non-consensual use of another person's body, and for all this the statement remains a recursive loop of prioritized axioms rather than an empirical truth.

## logical_tension

- **paradox**: The Identity Paradox: To grant the fetus 'adult' rights, one must ignore the very 'adult' (the mother) whose rights allow the fetus to exist.
- **ambiguity**: The term 'Person' acts as both a biological description and a moral award.
- **useful_contradiction**: The fetus is simultaneously 'part of the body' (biologically dependent) and 'a separate entity' (genetically distinct).

## reconclusion

The claim that a fetus possesses the same moral rights as an adult is a metaphysical axiom that serves as a foundational anchor for a specific ethical system; however, because it creates a direct logical collision with the established rights of adult bodily autonomy, it cannot be validated as a universal truth without an arbitrary prioritization of one life over another. It is an incomplete logical node.
