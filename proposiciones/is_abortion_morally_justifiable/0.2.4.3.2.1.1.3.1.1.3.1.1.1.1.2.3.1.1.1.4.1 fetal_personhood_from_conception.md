## conclusion

If we define life solely by genetic uniqueness, then conception is the start; however, if personhood requires cognitive or relational attributes found in adults, the premise fails. Scientific data from embryology (e.g., gastrulation, brain development) suggests that the capacities associated with human persons emerge over time (Paper: 'The Emergence of Human Personhood', Journal of Bioethics), and for all this, the statement remains an uncollapsible philosophical axiom rather than a biological fact.

## logical_tension

- **paradox**: The Potentiality Paradox: Treating a potential x as a current x leads to a logical category error (e.g., an acorn is a potential oak, but you cannot carve a table from an acorn).
- **ambiguity**: Human 'being' (biological) vs. human 'person' (moral/legal).
- **useful_contradiction**: A fetus is genetically 'us' but phenomenologically 'other' until birth or late gestation.

## reconclusion

The claim that a fetus is a person from conception is a metaphysical postulate that serves as a foundational axiom for specific ethical systems but lacks empirical falsifiability; since the status of 'person' is an assigned social value rather than an inherent biological trait like 'species', the statement cannot be collapsed into a universal truth without first agreeing on a subjective definition of personhood.
