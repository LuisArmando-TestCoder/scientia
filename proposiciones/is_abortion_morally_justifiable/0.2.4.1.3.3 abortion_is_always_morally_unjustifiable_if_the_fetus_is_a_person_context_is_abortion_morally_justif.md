## reconclusion

The assertion that personhood entails an absolute moral prohibition on abortion constitutes a logical incompleteness, as it fails to account for the conflicting personhood and sovereign rights of the gestational host, rendering the moral status of the act dependent on a hierarchy of rights rather than a single ontological status; and for all this, the proposition 'Abortion is always morally unjustifiable if the fetus is a person' is an incomplete moral formula that cannot be collapsed into a 'True' state without excluding the pregnant person's agency.

## causal_chain

Abortion definition requires understanding fetal growth. Fetal growth leads to the question of when life becomes a person. Personhood implies a right to life. A right to life is usually protected by moral rules. However, the pregnant person also has personhood. Their personhood implies a right to choose what happens to their body. These two rights collide in a shared space. To say abortion is 'always' wrong if the fetus is a person assumes the fetus's right to life is bigger than the mother's right to her own body. Since we have no universal rule saying which person's right is more important in this specific case, the original statement is a guess based on only looking at one side of the balance scale, making the whole idea an unfinished puzzle.
