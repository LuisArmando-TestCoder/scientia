## conclusion

If personhood is defined by biological DNA alone, then rights are inherent from conception, but because personhood in practice requires sentience or social integration (as evidenced by Judith Jarvis Thomson's 'Violinist' argument and the IVF embryo paradox), the claim of equivalent adult rights fails to collapse into a singular truth, and for all this abortion remains a conflict of competing rights rather than a violation of an established equivalence. And for all this, fetal personhood is a metaphysical claim, not a biological fact, rendering the equivalence false in pragmatic ethics.

## reconclusion

The assertion of fetal-adult moral equivalence is an axiomatic imposition rather than a derivative of first principles; because rights are reciprocal social constructs requiring a subject capable of interests, and a fetus lacks the neurological substrate for such interests until later development, the equivalence is structurally asymmetric and logically incomplete.

## causal_chain

Conception creates a unique genetic sequence. Some people call this sequence a person. If a person exists, they get a 'No Hurting' rule called a Right. If the fetus has this rule, it is as strong as the rule for grown-ups. If it is that strong, you can't stop the pregnancy because that would break the rule. But rules for grown-ups usually come from being able to think and feel. Since the fetus can't think or feel yet, the rule is being put there by people's ideas, not the fetus's own needs. Therefore, the rule only works if everyone agrees on the idea. Since not everyone agrees on when a person starts, the rule is not a solid wall but a choice people make based on their own beliefs.
