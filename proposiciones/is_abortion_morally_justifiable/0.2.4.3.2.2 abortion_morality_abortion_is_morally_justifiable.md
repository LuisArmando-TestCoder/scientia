## conclusion

If bodily autonomy is the primary right, then sovereignty over one's body implies the right to terminate assistance; if the fetus lacks sentient personhood, there is no competing moral agent; if legal access prevents greater systemic harm, it is a utilitarian good; and for all this the justification of abortion is a logical necessity within secular, autonomy-based frameworks despite remaining an irresolvable conflict for those who grant absolute personhood to zygotes. Abortion is justifiable when autonomy and welfare outweigh the claims of a potential but not yet actualized person.

## logical_tension

- **paradox**: A fetus is biologically 'human' but not necessarily 'a person', creating a gap between DNA and dignity.
- **ambiguity**: The term 'justifiable' varies depending on whether 'morality' is defined by individual rights or collective duties.
- **useful_contradiction**: The mother is both a single individual and a biological host for another, existing in a state of 'two-but-one' that defies standard individualistic logic.

## analogous_reorganization

- Compulsory organ donation: One cannot be forced to give blood/kidneys even to save a life, mirroring bodily autonomy.
- The Ship of Theseus: At what point do the cells become a 'person'?
- Ecosystem management: Balancing the needs of the environment (the mother) with the specific life within it.

## implications

- Moral justification is likely timing-dependent (gradualism).
- Legal systems will always mirror the underlying philosophical divide of their population.
- Technology (artificial wombs) may eventually decouple the rights of the fetus from the autonomy of the mother.

## reconclusion

The moral justifiability of abortion is logically sound under a hierarchy of rights that prioritizes the actualized consciousness and bodily sovereignty of the pregnant person over the potentiality of the fetus, provided that the 'personhood threshold' has not been quantitatively exceeded via sentience.

## preconceptual_reconclusion

The person who is already here and can feel has the biggest say, but as the baby-to-be grows and can feel too, we have to treat it with more and more care.

## causal_chain

Recognizing that human beings have rights because they have conscious interests leads to the conclusion that a non-conscious entity cannot have rights in the same way, which combined with the principle that one person is not a life-support machine for another without consent, makes the act of stopping a pregnancy a movement of self-determination rather than an act of malice, which is further validated by the fact that society is safer and healthier when individuals have this choice, and as these causal links connect autonomy to well-being, they justify the action by prioritizing the existing mind over the emerging biological process.
