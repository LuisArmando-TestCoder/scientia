## input_semantic_node

Fetal Personhood from Conception

## central_semantic_node

The Ontological Continuity of Moral Status from Zygote to Person

## argument_reasons

- Biological Continuity: The zygote contains a unique genetic code distinct from the parents, suggesting an individual life.
- Potentiality Argument: If nurtured, the fetus inevitably becomes a recognized person, implying inherent personhood.
- Moral Consistency: To avoid arbitrary benchmarks (size, dependence, sentience), conception is used as the only non-arbitrary starting point.

- **tension**: The gap between biological life ('human') and moral-legal status ('person').
- **limit**: Dependent on the axiomatic definition of 'personhood'.

## conclusion

If we define personhood as an essential quality linked to unique genetic existence, then the fetus must be granted full rights from conception, yet this conflicts with the biological reality of twinning and the autonomy of the host, and for all this the assertion remains a non-falsifiable philosophical axiom rather than a verifiable biological truth.

## logical_tension

- **paradox**: The Potentiality Paradox: Treating a potential x as an actual x before it has the properties of x.
- **ambiguity**: The term 'Human' refers to species, while 'Person' refers to a moral agent; the argument conflates the two.
- **useful_contradiction**: The fetus is biologically 'self' (unique DNA) but physiologically 'part' (dependent on the mother's organs).

## reconclusion

The claim of fetal personhood at conception is an foundational axiom that, while biologically grounded in genetic uniqueness, is philosophically arbitrary; its validity depends entirely on whether one prioritizes 'biological potential' or 'functional agency' as the source of rights, leading to an intractable conflict between two internally consistent but mutually exclusive moral systems.
