## conclusion

If ontology defines personhood as a biological byproduct, then the fetus is a person; if ethics defines personhood through consciousness or social interaction, it is not; and for all this, the question of abortion ethics is an irresolvable clash between the sanctity of biological life and the sovereignty of individual agency. Abortion cannot be evaluated without choosing a side of this fundamental dualism.

## logical_tension

- **paradox**: The Potentiality Paradox: A thing cannot be given the rights of what it is not yet, but failing to protect it prevents it from ever becoming that thing.

- **useful_contradiction**: The fetus is simultaneously part of the carrier's body and a separate genetic entity, making it a unique ontological state that escapes standard binary logic.

## reconclusion

The debate is not about when life begins, but about when 'we' decide a life matters; for all this, 'Personhood' is a tool of inclusion or exclusion used by systems to regulate behavior, and its application to a zygote is an attempt to use biology to settle a purely metaphysical dispute.

## causal_chain

Conception initiates a unique genetic sequence which establishes the potential for human consciousness; this potentiality is framed as inherently valuable by essentialists who then map legal 'personhood' onto the biological presence of DNA; this mapping creates a moral burden of protection identical to that of an adult human; if this burden is absolute, any termination of the process is categorized as the ending of a person's life; however, personhood is also defined by functional capacities like sentience and autonomy which the fetus lacks; thus the justification of abortion relies on the rejection of the genetic definition of a person in favor of a functional one, creating a terminal conflict between the right to exist and the right to self-determine because both claims use the same word 'right' to mean entirely different things based on entirely different starting ideas about what we are.
