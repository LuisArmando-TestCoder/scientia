## conclusion

If personhood is defined by the actualization of the thalamocortical complex necessary for self-awareness, then since a fetus lacks this hardware until late second trimester, it does not meet the functional criteria for a person; however, if moral justifiability is anchored in bodily autonomy (as suggested by the 'Violinist Argument' by Judith Jarvis Thomson), the personhood of the fetus becomes irrelevant to the right of the pregnant individual to terminate the pregnancy, and for all this, the proposition that a fetus possesses self-awareness is empirically false in early development, leading to the conclusion that developmental abortion is morally justifiable within a framework prioritizing actualized personhood or bodily autonomy.

## reconclusion

Given that self-awareness is a high-level cognitive function requiring integrated cortical activity, and such activity is absent in a fetus for the majority of gestation, the fetus does not possess self-awareness during the period when most abortions occur. If moral personhood is predicated on this awareness, then the fetus does not qualify as a person in that stage, rendering abortion justifiable under a personhood-based ethical system.
