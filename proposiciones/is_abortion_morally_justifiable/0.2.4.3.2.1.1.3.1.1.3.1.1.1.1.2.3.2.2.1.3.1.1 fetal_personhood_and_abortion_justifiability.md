## reconclusion

The statement 'a fetus is a person from conception' is a recursive definition used to anchor a moral outcome; however, because rights are inherently social and relational, an entity that cannot exist outside the biology of another cannot possess an 'equivalent' set of rights without negating the rights of the host, leading to a logical collapse of the concept of individual autonomy.

## causal_chain

Biological life begins at fertilization through the fusion of gametes creating a unique genome. This biological uniqueness is often mapped onto the philosophical concept of personhood. If personhood is granted at conception, the fetus enters the moral community as a rights-bearer. Among these rights, the right to life is considered primary. However, this right to life is enacted within the physical body of another person (the mother). A right to life does not usually include the right to use another person's body against their will (principle of bodily integrity). Therefore, even if the fetus is a person, the justifiability of abortion depends on the hierarchy of rights between two persons in a biological superposition. Since no other legal person is required to sacrifice their body to sustain another, granting the fetus such a right would grant it MORE rights than an adult, not just the same rights. Thus, the premise that 'equal rights' forbids abortion is logically inconsistent with the legal definition of equal rights for adults. and for all this, the question of abortion is not solved by personhood but by the limits of what one person can demand from the body of another.
