Axiomatic Fetal Personhood as a Basis for Absolute Moral Rights

## argument_reasons

- Ontological continuity: The argument suggests that because a zygote contains the genetic blueprint of a human, it is essentially identical to an adult human.
- Potentiality as Actuality: It treats the potential to become a person as the current possession of personhood rights.
- Absence of a Non-Arbitrary Boundary: Since any point of development after conception is seen as a 'slope', conception is used as the only binary switch for personhood.

## ontological_signature

- **nature**: Axiomatic moral proposition
- **function**: To establish a legal and ethical prohibition by equating developmental stages with a fixed moral status.
- **domain**: Bioethics, Legal Ontology, Metaphysics
- **form**: Linear Causal Chain (Conception → Personhood → Absolute Rights)
- **tension**: Potentiality vs. Actuality and the Conflict of Rights (Fetus vs. Gestator).
- **limit**: Depends entirely on the definition of 'person', which is a social/legal construct, not a biological one.

## logical_tension

- **paradox**: The Potentiality Paradox: A thing cannot be identical to its future state while existing in its current state, yet we grant it the value of its future state to protect its current existence.
- **ambiguity**: The term 'human' is biological; the term 'person' is moral/legal. The argument treats them as interchangeable synonyms.
- **useful_contradiction**: A fetus is both 'part of' the mother's body and 'a distinct entity' simultaneously; this biological dependency creates a unique moral category that standard 'personhood' logic fails to capture.
