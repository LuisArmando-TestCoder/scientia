## causal_chain

Determining the moral status of an action requires a fixed set of criteria for the subjects involved in that action. Personhood is proposed as the primary criterion for assigning rights to a fetus. If a fetus is granted personhood, it is assigned a right to life equivalent to that of the mother. If the right to life is considered absolute, then abortion becomes logically unjustifiable. However, personhood is not a factual observation but a value-laden assignment. Different systems assign personhood at different stages (conception, 24 weeks, birth). The lack of a biological constant for personhood means the justifiability of the act is not dependent on the fetus itself but on the legal/philosophical framework of the observer. Furthermore, even if personhood is granted, the 'Violinist' thought experiment demonstrates that a right to life does not inherently include the right to use another's body. Therefore, the dependency on personhood is a simplification of a more complex network of competing autonomous interests. The argument is therefore a pointer to a deeper conflict between the definition of a 'person' and the definition of 'ownership' over one's biological self. Since neither of these are solved by first principles, the original proposition serves as an anchor for a debate rather than a solution to it. The conclusion must remain undefined until a higher-order axiom is established to weight competing rights.
