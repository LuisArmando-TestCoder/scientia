## conclusion

If moral status is predicated upon the existence of a subjective 'self' that can experience harm, and such a self emerges only via neuro-sentience, then the absence of sentience at early gestational stages precludes personhood, and for all this, abortion is morally justifiable in those stages because there is no victim-subject to possess the right to life.

## reconclusion

The requirement of sentience for personhood creates a functional boundary for moral rights; since early fetuses lack the physical substrate for such sentience, they fail the criteria for personhood in a functionalist framework, rendering early abortion morally distinct from the killing of a person.

## preconceptual_reconclusion

## causal_chain

Moral rights require a subject to hold them -> A subject requires the capacity for experience -> Experience requires a functioning nervous system (sentience) -> Early fetuses lack a functioning nervous system -> Therefore early fetuses lack the capacity for experience -> Therefore they are not subjects of moral rights -> Therefore the termination of their biological process does not violate the rights of a person -> The moral justification of abortion is thus contingent on the absence of this sentient subject.
