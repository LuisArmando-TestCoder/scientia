## input_semantic_node

Fetal Personhood and Abortion Justifiability

## central_semantic_node

The Moral Equivalence of Fetal and Adult Human Rights

## argument_reasons

- Biological continuity argument: Life begins at conception as a unique genetic entity.
- Potentiality argument: A fetus is a 'potential' adult, thus possessing future-value rights.
- Inherent dignity argument: Personhood is an intrinsic quality of human life regardless of developmental stage.
- Justification for absence of functionalist arguments: Functionalist views (consciousness/viability) are omitted from the core premise because the premise assumes personhood *at* conception, bypassing developmental milestones.

## ontological_signature

- **nature**: Axiomatic moral proposition
- **function**: To establish a legal and ethical barrier against the termination of pregnancy.
- **domain**: Bioethics, Legal Ontology, Moral Philosophy.
- **form**: Binary status assignment (Person/Non-person).
- **tension**: Conflict between biological life and functional personhood; conflict between fetal rights and bodily autonomy.
- **limit**: Dependent on the subjective definition of 'Personhood', which is not empirically observable.

## conclusion

If personhood is defined by genetic uniqueness, and moral rights are absolute from that moment, then abortion is the killing of a person, yet the inability to empirically prove personhood or solve the hierarchy of conflicting rights renders the premise uncollapsible into a universal truth; and for all this, the statement remains a matter of axiomatic belief rather than logical fact.

## logical_tension

- **paradox**: The Potentiality Paradox: A zygote is a person because of what it will become, but it cannot exercise any rights intrinsic to personhood.
- **ambiguity**: The shift between 'Human Being' (biological) and 'Person' (moral/legal).
- **useful_contradiction**: The entity is biologically human but functionally pre-conscious, forcing a split between biological existence and moral value.

## analogous_reorganization

- The Sorites Paradox: At what point do grains of sand become a 'heap'? (At what point does a cell-mass become a 'person'?)
- The Violinist Thought Experiment (Judith Jarvis Thomson): Rights to life vs. rights to bodily support.

## implications

- If conception equals personhood, miscarriages may require legal investigation to ensure no rights were violated.
- Legal systems must redefine 'individual' to include non-autonomous biological entities.

## reconclusion

The claim that a fetus is a person from conception is a foundational axiom for a specific moral system, which, if accepted, logically necessitates the prohibition of abortion, but because personhood is a normative status and not a biological property, the argument cannot achieve formal global validity across all cognitive frameworks.
