## causal_chain

Determining moral rights requires a gatekeeping criterion usually defined as sentience because sentience provides the capacity for suffering which moral systems seek to minimize. A fetus at conception lacks a nervous system and thus lacks the capacity for sentience as a biological prerequisite. However if personhood is defined metabolically or genetically rather than neurologically then the fetus enters the protective category of 'person' immediately upon the creation of unique DNA. If this personhood is granted then the fetus possesses the same right to life as any other person. This creates a direct conflict with the mother's right to bodily autonomy which is the right to not have one's body used as a life-support system without consent. In standard person-to-person ethics no person can be forced to donate blood or organs to save another person even if that person will die without them. Therefore even if the fetus is a person the mother's right to withdraw bodily support remains a valid application of the autonomy axiom. Consequently the only way to ban abortion is to argue that the fetus has a 'super-right' to life that exceeds the autonomy rights of the mother or to claim the mother has a unique duty of care that overrides her own autonomy. Because there is no empirical way to prove personhood over sentience or vice versa the system remains in a state of logical superposition where the choice of starting axiom dictates the inevitable outcome of the entire moral chain.
