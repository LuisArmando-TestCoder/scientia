## conclusion

If personhood is defined by cognitive function (sentience) as argued by Giubilini and Minerva (2012), then the zygote lacks the ontological status of a person; if bodily autonomy is an absolute right as per Thomson (1971), even a fetal person cannot demand the use of another's body, and for all this, the morality of abortion is a conflict of incommensurable rights that cannot be resolved by a single biological fact since personhood is a socially and philosophically constructed status. ## logical_tension

- **paradox**: The Potentiality Paradox: Treating a potential person as an actual person creates rights for a being that doesn't yet have the capacity to exercise them.
- **ambiguity**: The term 'life' conflates 'biological metabolic activity' with 'biographical identity'.
- **useful_contradiction**: A fetus is simultaneously 'part of' the pregnant person (biologically) and 'distinct from' them (genetically).

## analogous_reorganization

- A blueprint vs. a house: the blueprint contains the instructions, but you cannot live in it.

## implications

- If life's value is purely genetic, then every cell with DNA has potential moral weight.
- Legal systems must decide between 'biological absolutism' and 'functional pragmatism'.

## reconclusion

The claim that a fetus is a person from conception is a metaphysical axiom that creates a rigid moral system; however, because personhood requires relational or cognitive traits not present at conception, the logic of 'rights' remains suspended in a state of irresolvable social disagreement.

## preconceptual_reconclusion
